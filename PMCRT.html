What We've Done

Results

We have taken solid-tumor cancer patients and infused expanded Tumor-Infilitrating Lymphocytes (TILs). We then tracked the response using the newly developed technique CapTCR and tracked specific CDR3 sequences over time to predict patient response.

#1: Tracking Immunotherapy Through Blood Samples

We previously described a capture-based TCR sequencing method (CapTCR) and it was further developed through the project to increase efficacy and efficiency. Through this project, I have been able to track specific cancer-targetting clonotypes infused during immunotherapy and how a patient's immune response reacts to it. This allows for non-invasive monitoring of next-generation immunotherapies.

#2: Effect of TIL Infusion Clonality on Patient Response

Past studies have proposed that increased diversity and decreased clonality will increase the anti-tumor immune response after immunotherapy. Through my analysis, I found that there is no direct correlation between the clonality of the TIL Infusion Product and the patient's response to the therapy.

#3: Selected TIL Expansion

Through tracking specific clones over time we discovered that, contrary to prior belief, it is not the largest clones in the TIL infusion that will expand in a lymphopenic envioronment. We found that although the patients immune system has been chemodepleted, it is possible that many HP-memory cells exist and are controlling the selection of TIL infusion clones to expand.

#4: Baseline and Post-infusion Overlap

A correlation was found with the repertoire overlap between samples. For the low response patients, it was found that the patients who had a high similarity between their baseline and post-infusion samples. What this shows is that patients who had an inefficient chemodepletion, took up less of the TIL Infusion.
